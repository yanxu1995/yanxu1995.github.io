Design Process

SYSTEMS DEVELOPMENT LIFE CYCLE

In the first part, we will briefly describe two typical system design processes used by many different industries.
We will talk about Waterfall Model of Design at first.

Waterfall Model of Design

The Waterfall Model systems development life cycle is depicted as a series of stages, each of which flows into the next.
This means that you need to clear most of the tasks from the previous stage before moving on to the next stage.

Next, let’s take a look at the Concurrent Engineering Model.

Concurrent Engineering Model

Sometimes, the systems development life cycle processes do not occur in neat phases.
unlike the Waterfall Model of Design,
in Concurrent Engineering Model, each phase overlaps considerably as engineers work concurrently through analysis, design, testing, and development.

IPT is a team composed of individuals from many different specialties (e.g., marketing, manufacturing, sales, and engineering) who bring different issues to bear on the process.

Companies can make products easier to manufacture or maintain by using IPT and Concurrent Engineering to eliminate or minimize costly redesigns.

As one kind of Concurrent Engineering Model, Spiral design model is a design process that
test the Early prototypes and feed back the results to the ongoing design process for further modification,
additional technical analysis and research will be conducted When vulnerabilities and weaknesses are identified or new technologies emerge in the marketplace.

The software industry is also seeing more and more deployments of early versions of software (beta or sometimes first release), even before the final code is completed.
However, this is rarely seen for high-importance systems such as aviation, driving, or power plant control.
As a upside of this approach, products can be brought to market more quickly. As a downside, beta deployments severely neglect system users.

Finally, the author emphasizes that In User-centered Design, deployments of early versions should be avoided, to prevent frustrating and perhaps alienating current and potential system users.

USER INTERFACE DESIGN PROCESS

In the second part,
We will discuss how the SA requirements of the system operator are addressed in this process,

First we will talk about the Requirements Analysis, which is a part of the design requirements in UI design process.

Requirements Analysis

Requirements analysis is perhaps one of the most important phases of the design process.

Operational Concept

Generally, the designers develop an operational concept that describes their vision for how the proposed system will be used.

Such a concept would include the types of missions or functions that should be performed using the system,
the capabilities the system should bestow on the user
and the Operational constraints.

The operational concept Provides important background on the intended use and functions of the system.

Environmental Conditions

In addition to the operational concept, at this phase,
it is very important to define the environmental conditions in which the system will be used.
This will often include consideration of factors such as

  • Ambient noise levels
  • Lighting levels
  • Susceptibility to weather and temperature variations
  • Vibration
  • Privacy
  • Security requirements
  • Expected pace of operations
  • Positions of use (e.g., sitting, standing, while mobile)
  • Workload and stress levels
  • Frequency of use (e.g., occasional, intermittent, frequent, continuous)
  • The need to integrate with other current systems
  • Unusual characteristics in the working environment such as weightlessness or environmental toxicity

User Characteristics

The types of users the system accommodates and their pertinent characteristics ,
which means user characteristics.
should be listed up at this phase.

This should include a description of

  • Gender (male, female, or both)
  • Sizes, including height and weight (percentile of the population to be
    accommodated)
  • Skill levels, training, and background knowledge (including reading capability and experience with similar types of systems)
  • Age ranges (with special note of young or aging populations)
  • Visual acuity and hearing capabilities
  • Languages to be accommodated
  • Special clothing or other equipment to be accommodated (such as gloves,
    masks, or backpacks)
  • Any physical disabilities or special requirements
  • The need to accommodate multiple users on the same system

Operational Requirements

Finally, the operational requirements for system users should be specified.
which includes How the users work and operate in their jobs,
The types of physical and cognitive processes they employ,
Their need for interaction with others,
and Their SA requirements.

Typically, we can conduct operational requirements through a CTA, which means cognitive task analysis.

There are many variants of CTA,
and Most use one or a combination of Observations of operations in representative similar environments,
Interviews with subject matter experts (SMEs),
and Reviews of available documentation from the domain of interest.

Together the operational concept, environmental constraints, user characteristics, and operational requirements form the basic input to the design process.

and then, These documents must then be translated into a set of implementable system requirements.
By translating the human requirements into system requirements,
many problems downstream can be avoided.

Typical engineering requirements for a system might be that data is transferred across the network within 6s and there is a 90% success rate on such transitions.
Such requirements completely ignore the human processing component, however, Users could be swamped with data that is poorly presented.
suitable system requirements that are more user-centric might include a requirement for changes in key information to be detected by users within 2s, 
or for the user to be able to demonstrate SA of 90% of high priority messages.

Technology Analysis

Technology analysis is generally conducted simultaneously with requirements analysis,
and frequently is an ongoing activity in many companies and organizations.

Technology analysis typically incorporates an ongoing survey of products in the market
that are relevant to the product line of the company
and may include things new sensors, computer architectures, and so on.

Many companies have ongoing internal research and development (IR&D) programs
to actively develop new technological capabilities in these areas.

The technology analysis forms a second important contribution to the design process
by ascertaining the range of devices and their characteristics.

Design Conceptualization

After introducing the requirements part, Lets dive into the Design part, Design Conceptualization。
There are several different activities that occur during the design concept development phase.
such as Function Analysis/Function Allocation,
User Interface Design
and the Design Concept Products.

First, we will talk about the Function Analysis, and Function Allocation.

Function Analysis/Function Allocation

the function Analysis is determining the basic functions that need to be performed to accomplish the job,
and the function allocation is then performed that specifies who will do each function (human, machine, or a combination of both).

Traditionally, lists were developed that provided designers with recommendations on
what sort of tasks were best performed by humans and by machines.
Such approaches have largely fallen out of favor.
Sanders & McCormick said the Reason is

  1. technology has developed to such an extent that computers are capable of many tasks that previously only people could perform, blurring the lines of distinction between the two.
  2. A general recognition that very few tasks are really performed solely by either humans or machines.

User Interface Design

then we will talk about the User Interface Design.

About User Interface Design，the Pre-existing practices is
They have an application of a well-developed set of human factors principles and design guidelines
that allow designers to avoid many, many pitfalls in that design process.

These guidelines have been developed based on half a century of scientific testing
and empirical data collection that establishes how to best design system components to enhance human use.

which primarily focused on the perceptual and physical design of systems， Compensate for various environmental issues， and Physical design of workspaces.

The application that we mentioned before is very useful,
but it has some Limitations according to the author.

First, these guidelines exist, for the most part, at the level of the component.
SA, by its very nature, is highly affected by not only the design of individual system components,
but by how those components are integrated.

Second, these guidelines are primarily focused on the perceptual and physical attributes of humans,
but not on their cognitive attributes.
Just as we need to design chairs to fit the human body, we also need to design systems to support the way people take in and process information.

thankfully, Fields of endeavor such as cognitive engineering
and naturalistic decision making have done much to help build a foundation of knowledge on human cognition.

Design Concept Products

Finally, lets talk about the Design Concept Products.
The products of this design process can be expressed in many ways such as

  • Flow analysis or operational sequence diagrams
  • Task analysis
  • Timeline analysis
  • Link Analysis
  • UI specification

A Flow analysis or operational sequence diagrams can describe Decision sequences used by the system and/or user．
this is an example from the Internet.
Describes the flow of customers from the time they enter the cat cafe store to the time they leave.

A Task analysis is Lists the actual tasks that the user must perform with the system.
the example is a Task analysis Lists for surgery reception duties.

A Timeline analysis is The tasks that must be performed along a timeline.

A Link analysis A network of interrelationships among elements of the design,
most typically different controls and displays.
this is an example of Link analysis.

A UI specification is A document that includes text, graphics, and often flow charts showing the displays and controls of the UI.
this is the example document from the internet.

Many people think of the UI as something that comes after the engineers have designed the physical system
and are then designed as a front end to allow human interaction and operation.
But in reality, in the authors’ view,
The UI needs to be specified very early in the design phase.

because, one,
The effectiveness of the overall system is largely determined by
not just the physical nature of the displays and controls,
but also by the inherent functionality of the system, such as UI.
and, two,
Needed changes to the system design that become apparent
when the UI is developed at the end of the project are also quite expensive and difficult,
and often simply never occur.

Prototype Development

The development of models, simulations, and prototypes of design concepts (and variants of design concepts)
that are conceptualized in the design phase is very useful.
In addition to providing a common frame of reference for discussion,
these prototypes provide the basis for detecting design problems and shortcomings early in the design process.

We will discuss each progressive level of prototyping in more detail.

Modeling

Many types of models exist to help in evaluating UI design concepts.
includes the models that Evaluating physical layout of the system,
workload issues,
man-loading, training, and personnel issues,
Predicting human information processing characteristics
and so on.

through these models,
Many variations of design concepts can be evaluated fairly easily,
and we can Examining specific aspects of the design early in the design phase.

but,
It takes considerable time and effort to adequately model the tasks, design concept characteristics, and features of the system,
It also can be quite difficult to validate the quality of the models
and,
Although the physical models available are fairly good,
the cognitive models remain fairly limited
in terms of the number of components of human cognition considered
and the degree to which good predictions of human performance can be made from them.

Rapid Prototyping

and then,
after we Modeling the UI design concepts.
we can move on to the Rapid Prototyping.

Rapid Prototyping allows us to make a more realistic assessment of their viability,
by quickly mocking up potential function allocations and displaying ideas on a computer.

What is most important is that
these prototypes are created early,
allowing needed design input from engineers and users
so that design convergence occurs.
through Rapid Prototyping,
we can get subjective comments provided by knowledgeable users

But Rapid Prototyping is limited in terms of
assessments of complete integrated systems
for more complex situations.
In these cases, simulation testing is usually required.

Simulation Testing

Simulations Testing provide detailed, objective data
on the implications of different design decisions early in the design process
when changes can be made easily
and without the excessive costs that arise from finding problems later in the field.
through Simulation,
we can do objective test and evaluating by detailed data.

Field Tests

Field-testing provides the advantage of greater realism in the environmental conditions and scenarios that can be considered. The data collected often has a high degree of face validity because of this.

but, on the other hand,
it is much harder to achieve experimental control in a field test.
and,
Field Tests require working functional prototypes of systems,
This generally means that one will be far down the development cycle
before such testing can take place.

In summary,
a field test is quite appropriate
late in the design process
to examine how the system will be used in realistic situations and conditions.

Test and Evaluation

Test and Evaluation depends largely on having a good battery of measurement tools,
This Test and Evaluation section refers to measurement tools that include
Subjective Measures, Objective Measures, Workload Measures and Situation Awareness Measures.
And it highlights the evaluation metrics can assess user processes, cognitive states, and performance at different stages.(Figure 4.6).

Subjective Measures

At first, lets learn about the Subjective Measures.

Subjective input by users can provide useful information.
Such as missing features, confusing labeling, conditions of use not well supported by the design, and so on.

The biggest limitation of subjective data is that
many problems with system designs that can critically affect human performance
will not be obvious to naïve users.
they are only revealed by careful testing and objective data collection.

Objective Measures of Performance

Then Objective Measures of Performance.

Objective performance measurement usually takes the form of
measures of effectiveness (MOEs) or
measures of performance (MOPs).

MOEs generally include measures of the overall performance of the combined human/machine system.
MOPs are usually more granular and focus on particular subsystems.

When using OBjective performance measurement,
we need to pay attention to the following points.

Objective Measures of Performance may be impossible
to conduct a sufficient number of test scenarios
to examine what would happen to performance under many potential conditions.

Often times significant improvements in designs will not be detected based on performance measures alone.
For very new types of systems,
users may not yet have developed good strategies and tactics
for best using new systems to maximize performance objectives.

Users often ,
will try very hard to achieve good performance
and may work harder to compensate for poor designs.
Over time, however, in real operations,
such design problems will lead to errors and degraded performance.

Performance measures are often not sufficiently granular
or diagnostic of differences in system designs.

Workload Measures

The next measures is Workload Measures.

High user workload has a higher probability of overload and error,
given multiple events and demands in the operational environment.
By directly measuring user workload,
design concepts that are less demanding can be selected during design testing.

but, In other systems,
low workload is also a concern.
Operations requiring long periods of vigilance (where very little happens or changes) can be degrading to SA and user performance.

Here the author gives us some different approaches to workload measurement,
such as Physiological measures, Subjective measures, and so on.

Situation Awareness Measures

using the SA measures,
Problems with SA,
brought on by data overload, nonintegrated data, automation, complex systems that are poorly understood, excess attention demands, and many other factors,
can be detected early in the design process and corrective changes made to improve the design.

and,
Direct measurement of SA during prototyping and simulation
has been found to be very sensitive to design differences
that performance measures alone could not detect during testing .

Measurement of SA also provides good Diagnostic,
allowing designers to detect exactly why a given design may have fared well or poorly.
or in terms of which aspects of SA may have improved or declined with a particular design concept.

Here the author gives us some approaches for SA measurement,
such as Process measures, Performance measures and so on.

Final Design

Finally, the final design.

before the final Design,
early prototyping and simulation testing to find as many design shortcomings as possible are crucial.

after the final design,
continued monitoring of
how users actually use the system in real operational conditions after implementation
is very important.
